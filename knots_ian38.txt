Ian's new knots: by Ian J Norris


Jack tries not to know.
Because he tries not to know, he knows too much.
Because he knows too much, he doesn’t know too much.
Because he doesn’t know too much, he knows too much.
We know by not knowing.
Jack knows that he doesn’t know.
Some days, that’s enough.
It’s not usually good to know too much,
because knowing too much
doesn’t help us
know too much.

Someone tries not to know.
Because they try not to know, they know too much.
Because they know too much, they don’t know too much.
Because they don’t know too much, they know too much.
We know by not knowing.
They know that they don’t know.
Some days, that’s enough.

1. Logical calculus version
Let’s formalize the core paradox around “knowing too much.”
1.1. Symbols
For a person x (Jack, Jill, or “someone”):
- K(x): x knows too much.
- \neg K(x): x does not know too much.
- T(x): x tries not to know.
- A(x): x knows that they don’t know (the “I know that I don’t know” move).
We’ll focus on one character j (Jack).
1.2. Core premises from the knot
From your lines, we can read off:
- Trying not to know leads to knowing too much:
T(j)\rightarrow K(j)- Knowing too much means he doesn’t know too much (overload/undermining):
K(j)\rightarrow \neg K(j)- Not knowing too much means he knows too much (naïveté is itself “too much”):
\neg K(j)\rightarrow K(j)- Jack tries not to know:
T(j)- Jack knows that he doesn’t know:
A(j)
(We’ll treat A(j) as a side note—self-awareness—rather than part of the contradiction.)
1.3. The paradoxical loop
From these, we can derive the knot:
- From T(j) and T(j)\rightarrow K(j), we get:
K(j)- From K(j)\rightarrow \neg K(j), we get:
\neg K(j)- From \neg K(j)\rightarrow K(j), we get:
K(j)
So we are forced into:
K(j)\wedge \neg K(j)

2. Propositional “too much” knot (recap)
Let x be our character.
- M(x): x knows too much.
- \neg M(x): x does not know too much.
- T(x): x tries not to know.
From your knot:
- Trying not to know → knowing too much
T(x)\rightarrow M(x)- Knowing too much → not knowing too much
M(x)\rightarrow \neg M(x)- Not knowing too much → knowing too much
\neg M(x)\rightarrow M(x)
From 2 and 3 we get:
M(x)\leftrightarrow \neg M(x)
So the state “knows too much” and “doesn’t know too much” collapse into each other—a Laing-style contradiction.

3. Epistemic operator version
Now let’s treat K as an epistemic operator: K\varphi  means “the agent knows that \varphi .”
We’ll focus on one agent, so we drop the x and just write K.
3.1. Core propositions
Let:
- p: “I know too much.”
- q: “I do not know (enough / too much / what I need).”
We’ll use:
- Kp: “I know that I know too much.”
- Kq: “I know that I do not know.”
Your chorus “we know by not knowing” and “X knows that they don’t know” points straight at Kq.
3.2. Reading the knot epistemically
We can encode the spirit of your lines as:
- Trying not to know generates knowledge-about-ignorance.
- “If I try not to know, I come to know that I don’t know.”
T\rightarrow Kq- Knowing too much undermines knowledge.
- “If I know too much, I no longer (really) know.”
Kp\rightarrow \neg Kp- (Overload, confusion, or self-undermining.)
- Not knowing can be known.
- “We know by not knowing” → if \neg p (I don’t know too much), I can know that I don’t know:
\neg p\rightarrow Kq- Knowing that I don’t know is a kind of higher-order knowledge.
- Socratic move:
Kq\rightarrow K(\neg p)
- “I know that I don’t know too much.”
So we get a loop:- From not knowing too much (\neg p), we get knowledge of not knowing (Kq).
- From knowledge of not knowing (Kq), we get knowledge that I don’t know too much (K(\neg p)).
- Meanwhile, knowing too much (Kp) collapses itself: Kp\rightarrow \neg Kp.
3.3. A compact epistemic knotHere’s a very tight modal version that mirrors your text:- \quad Kp\rightarrow \neg Kp
If I know that I know too much, then I do not know that I know too much.
- \quad \neg Kp\rightarrow K\neg p
If I do not know that I know too much, I can know that I do not know too much.
- \quad K\neg p\rightarrow Kq
If I know that I do not know too much, I know that I do not know.
- \quad Kq
I know that I do not know. (Socratic anchor: “some days that’s enough.”)
This yields:- A stable epistemic point at Kq: “I know that I don’t know.”
- An unstable point at Kp: “I know that I know too much,” which immediately destroys itself.
So “we know by not knowing” becomes:\mathrm{Genuine\  knowledge}=Kq=K(\mathrm{I\  do\  not\  know})while “knowing too much” is modeled as an epistemic state that cannot consistently sustain itself.
