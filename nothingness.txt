. What Sartre is really doing with Pierre
In Being and Nothingness, the famous “Pierre is not in the café” scene isn’t just:

For Sartre, that absence is structured by a prior expectation or project: you went there for Pierre. The café is “full” of his absence because your consciousness is already oriented toward finding him.
So phenomenologically, the real structure is closer to:

where:
• 	: Pierre is in the café (now).
• 	: Pierre was supposed to be here (you came expecting him).
Only when both are in play—he’s not there, and he was supposed to be—do you get the charged, Sartrean “nothingness.”

2. A simple truth table for Sartre’s Pierre
Let’s start with just the core pair: “is he here?” and “was he supposed to be here?”
• 	: Pierre is in the café (now).
• 	: Pierre was supposed to be here (now).
• 	: “Sartrean negation”: the meaningful “Pierre is not here” as an experienced absence.
Define:

Truth table:

| \(p\) (here) | \(s\) (supposed) | \(\lnot p\) | \(N = \lnot p \land s\) | Phenomenological reading |
|--------------|------------------|------------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| T            | T                | F          | F                        | He’s here as expected; no “nothingness.” |
| T            | F                | F          | F                        | He’s here, but you weren’t expecting him; surprise, not absence. |
| F            | T                | T          | **T**                    | The Sartre case: he’s missing *and* expected → “Pierre’s absence” saturates the café. |
| F            | F                | T          | F                        | He’s not here, but you had no expectation; just neutral non-presence. |

So the “comic” bit, logically, is that Sartre’s grand nothingness cashes out as a very ordinary conjunction:

But phenomenologically, he’s saying: consciousness constitutes this negation—it’s not just a bare logical fact.

# 🌀 1. Define the predicates

We’ll use your five predicates:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **\(p\)** | Pierre is in the café (now) |
| **\(s\)** | Pierre was supposed to be here (you expected him) |
| **\(e\)** | Pierre has been in the café before |
| **\(f\)** | Pierre will be in the café in the future |
| **\(k\)** | Pierre knows the café exists |

And we define the **Sartrean-negation-with-bite** as:

\[
N := \lnot p \land s \land k
\]

This captures Sartre’s idea that absence becomes *charged* only when:

- he is **not** here,
- he **was supposed** to be here,
- he **knows** about the café (so his absence feels intentional, meaningful, or at least personally relevant).

The other variables \(e\) and \(f\) will shape the *flavor* of the absence.

# 🌀 4. What this table reveals about Sartre

Sartre’s “Pierre is not in the café” is not a simple negation.  
It’s a **structured phenomenon** that depends on:

- your project (expectation),
- Pierre’s relation to the café (knowledge, past, future),
- and the intentionality of consciousness.

The truth table makes visible what Sartre dramatizes:

> **Negation is not in the world; it is produced by consciousness.**

Only certain combinations of facts produce the *felt* nothingness.

Ian, this is where things get delicious.  
A **phenomenological algebra** is exactly the kind of structure Sartre *implies* but never formalizes. Let’s build one that treats **absence as a graded, relational, intentional phenomenon**—not a binary.

Below is a system that feels like it could have been smuggled out of *Being and Nothingness* by a logician.

---

# 🌑 1. Core Idea  
In classical logic, negation is binary:

\[
p = 1 \quad \text{or} \quad p = 0
\]

But phenomenologically, **absence has intensities** depending on:

- expectation  
- history  
- future projection  
- knowledge  
- emotional investment  
- narrative orientation  

So we define an **absence-intensity function**:

\[
A(p, s, e, f, k) \in [0,1]
\]

Where:

- \(0\) = pure neutrality (mere non-being)  
- \(1\) = full Sartrean nothingness (the café trembles with Pierre’s absence)

---

# 🌒 2. Define the Variables as Intensities  
Instead of True/False, each predicate becomes a value in \([0,1]\):

| Symbol | Meaning | Range |
|--------|---------|--------|
| \(p\) | Pierre’s presence (1 = fully present) | \([0,1]\) |
| \(s\) | Strength of expectation | \([0,1]\) |
| \(e\) | Weight of past presence | \([0,1]\) |
| \(f\) | Weight of future projection | \([0,1]\) |
| \(k\) | Degree of Pierre’s knowledge of the café | \([0,1]\) |

Presence becomes:

\[
\text{Absence} = 1 - p
\]

---

# 🌓 3. The Absence-Intensity Formula  
We define:

\[
A = (1 - p) \cdot s \cdot k \cdot \left(\alpha e + \beta f + \gamma\right)
\]

Where:

- \(\alpha\) = weight of the past  
- \(\beta\) = weight of the future  
- \(\gamma\) = baseline existential charge  

This captures Sartre’s idea that absence is **constituted** by consciousness, history, and projection.

### A simple, elegant version  
Let’s choose:

- \(\alpha = 0.3\)  
- \beta = 0.3  
- \gamma = 0.4  

So:

\[
A = (1 - p) \cdot s \cdot k \cdot (0.3e + 0.3f + 0.4)
\]

This ensures the past and future matter, but expectation and knowledge matter more.

---

# 🌔 4. Interpretations of Absence Levels

### **A ≈ 0.0 — Mere Non-Being**  
Pierre is not here, but nothing in the world is oriented toward him.  
The café is just a café.

### **A ≈ 0.2 — Ambient Absence**  
He’s not here, but he’s been here before.  
A faint ghost of presence.

### **A ≈ 0.4 — Narrative Absence**  
He’s not here, you expected him a little, he knows the café.  
The world feels slightly misaligned.

### **A ≈ 0.6 — Emotional Absence**  
You expected him strongly, he has a past here, and he knows the café.  
The absence has emotional texture.

### **A ≈ 0.8 — Existential Absence**  
He was supposed to be here, he knows the café, and he has a future here.  
The café feels *wrong* without him.

### **A = 1.0 — Sartrean Nothingness**  
The full Pierre effect:  
- he is not here  
- he was strongly expected  
- he knows the café  
- he has a past and future here  

The café is “full of Pierre’s absence.”
